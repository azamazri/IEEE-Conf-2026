\begin{abstract}
Content-based perfume recommenders rank products by order-one accord similarity, such as
cosine or Jaccard over note sets, and treat accords as independent. Whether modeling how
accords \emph{co-occur} improves recommendation has not been tested under a leakage-free
protocol, and most product catalogs record no user interactions, which rules out the
collaborative and knowledge-graph recommenders that need an interaction signal. This paper
studies interaction-free, content-based perfume recommendation as a retrieval task: given a
reference perfume, a catalog is ranked to surface the product that imitates it, and a
held-out inspired-by edge supplies ground-truth relevance. The proposed matcher is
parameter-free; it extends accord TF-IDF with higher-order co-occurrence tokens---accord
pairs and triples---and its cosine score decomposes additively by interaction order, while
emitting auditable co-occurrence paths for explanation. On 340 products and 209 reference
perfumes under leave-one-out ranking, behind a three-source firewall whose query accords are
independently verified from Fragrantica, accuracy improves as co-occurrence order increases:
mean reciprocal rank rises from 0.454 for the order-one baseline to 0.508 at order three,
which outranks twelve comparators spanning marginal similarity, neural embeddings,
perceptual-taxonomy transport, and learned models. The gain is modest but statistically
robust, and higher-order structure carries the majority of the ranking signal. The
contribution is a finding and a leakage-free evaluation protocol rather than a new model.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Aromatique is an Indonesian perfume brand that sells locally blended scents, many of them
crafted to smell like a well-known global perfume, across a growing network of branches.
During peak hours the recommendation load falls on the sales staff, who must match a
customer's reference scent to the right in-house product from memory; the brand needs an
automated recommender to relieve this bottleneck. Crucially, the catalog records only
content---each product's olfactory \emph{accords} (e.g., \textit{amber}, \textit{woody},
\textit{warm spicy}) and its family---and no clicks or ratings. The setting is therefore
\emph{interaction-free}: recommendations must be made from content alone.

Existing perfume recommenders are almost entirely content-based and \emph{order-one}: they
rank candidates by single-accord similarity using cosine over term frequency--inverse
document frequency (TF-IDF) vectors or Jaccard over accord sets. Lutan \cite{r23} surveys the
field, while Nurmuthia \textit{et al.} \cite{r24} and Fannisa \textit{et al.} \cite{r28} rely
on cosine, Jaccard, and TF-IDF over notes; none models how accords \emph{co-occur}. This is a
real limitation: two products can share the same individual accords with the reference yet
differ in which accords appear \emph{together}, so an order-one score ties the correct
imitation with a distractor, whereas higher-order structure separates them
(Fig.~\ref{fig:problem}).

Richer models are not directly available here. Interaction-driven recommenders that exploit
co-occurrence and higher-order connectivity---collaborative filtering, LightGCN, KGIN, and
KGAT \cite{r18,r19,r20}---report strong gains, but only in session or multi-behavior settings
\cite{r1,r2,r3,r4,r5}; they learn from a user--item signal this catalog does not contain.
Neural and learned embeddings, and perceptual-geometry priors from odor science
\cite{r10,r11,r12,r13}, are likewise attractive in principle, yet on small, imbalanced data
simple baselines routinely match or beat flexible models that overfit \cite{r14,r15,r16,r17}.
Whether accord \emph{co-occurrence} helps a content-only, interaction-free recommender---and
how far the benefit extends with interaction order---has not been tested under an evaluation
that cannot leak the answer.

This paper takes a deliberately simple route: it extends accord TF-IDF with
\emph{higher-order} co-occurrence tokens---accords, accord pairs, and accord triples, the
subsets of a fragrance's accord set---weighted by inverse document frequency and compared by
cosine, so the score decomposes additively by interaction order. The matcher carries no
trained parameters, which on a catalog this small is a design choice rather than a limitation,
since added capacity only overfits. The contribution is a \emph{finding and a protocol}, not a
new model, at a moderate applied scope:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Finding.} Accord co-occurrence improves interaction-free perfume retrieval over
the order-one baseline; the gain strengthens with interaction order and then saturates, so we
adopt order three as the operating point.
\item \textbf{Protocol.} A non-circular, leakage-free evaluation with a three-source firewall
(local item accords, externally sourced query accords, and held-out imitation labels used only
as ground truth), under which we also explain why interaction-based recommenders such as KGAT
cannot be compared on this data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Problem Formulation and Non-circular Protocol}\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Task and interaction order}
Let $\mathcal{P}=\{p_1,\dots,p_M\}$ be the catalog of local products, $M=340$; each product
$p$ has an accord set $A(p)\subseteq\mathcal{V}$ and a family label, where $\mathcal{V}$ is
the accord vocabulary. Let $\mathcal{Q}$ be the set of reference perfumes (branded
originals); each reference $q$ has accords $A(q)$. The imitation relation gives a
ground-truth set
\begin{equation}
g(q)=\{\,p\in\mathcal{P}\;:\;\textit{inspired\_by}(p)=q\,\}, \label{eq:gold}
\end{equation}
the products built to smell like $q$. A method scores every product, $s(q,p)$, ranks the
whole catalog, and is judged by the rank of the ground-truth product. Of the $340$ products,
$243$ carry a label and $97$ are unlabeled originals kept as genuine distractors; $209$
references have at least one labeled product and form the leave-one-out evaluation set.

The key quantity is \emph{interaction order}, the content-only analogue of the high-order
connectivity exploited by knowledge-graph recommenders \cite{r20}. The order-$k$ tokens of a
fragrance $f$ are its accord subsets of size $k$,
\begin{equation}
T_k(f)=\{\,S\subseteq A(f)\;:\;|S|=k\,\}, \label{eq:order}
\end{equation}
so order one is the single accords (marginal similarity), order two the co-occurring pairs,
and order three the co-occurring triples. A representation of order $N$ uses all tokens of
order $1\le k\le N$.

\subsection{Deployment versus evaluation}
The deployed recommender and the benchmark share an \emph{identical} scoring engine and
differ only in query source and in whether a ground-truth answer exists. In deployment the
query is a customer's free preference and any product may be returned---an in-house imitation
or an original. In evaluation the query is a reference perfume, because only there does a
held-out answer exist (the imitation edge); a reference perfume is never itself returned,
since the pool is the local catalog. The $97$ originals without a labeled imitation are thus
not discarded but retained in the pool as \emph{non-target} candidates, which makes the
retrieval realistically hard.

\subsection{A non-circular firewall}
The central methodological risk is circularity: if query features were copied from the
products they must retrieve, accord overlap would be a tautology. A three-source separation
guards against this (Table~\ref{tab:firewall}): item accords come from the local catalog,
query accords are sourced independently from Fragrantica ($264$ of $266$ \texttt{source\_url}
entries resolve to perfume-specific pages), and the imitation label is held out. Formally,
interaction-based recommenders (LightGCN, KGIN, KGAT) require a user--item matrix
$R\in\{0,1\}^{U\times I}$; here $U=\varnothing$, so $R$ is undefined and such models cannot be
instantiated. A leakage audit finally strips identity-bearing text before any text-based
method sees it: the reference name never appears, and distinctive name tokens found in the
free text of $32$ of $340$ products are removed.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}
The matcher represents a fragrance by its accords \emph{and} by which accords co-occur in it,
weights every token by rarity, and compares two fragrances with a single cosine that splits
additively by interaction order. It has no trained parameters. Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline} shows
the pipeline.

\subsection{Higher-order token representation}
Using the order-$k$ tokens of \eqref{eq:order}, the order-$N$ representation of a fragrance
$f$ pools all tokens up to order $N$,
\begin{equation}
T_{\le N}(f)=\bigcup_{k=1}^{N} T_k(f). \label{eq:tokens}
\end{equation}
Order one recovers the single accords of a plain TF-IDF baseline; orders two and three add the
co-occurring pairs and triples this work contributes.

\subsection{Weighting, normalization, and scoring}
Each token is weighted by inverse document frequency over the $M=340$ products, with document
frequency $\mathrm{df}(t)=|\{f:t\in T_{\le N}(f)\}|$:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{idf}(t)=\log\frac{1+M}{1+\mathrm{df}(t)}+1. \label{eq:idf}
\end{equation}
Rare co-occurrences receive a high weight while ubiquitous single accords receive a low one, so
the hub penalty is automatic and parameter-free. A fragrance becomes a sparse vector $v_f$ with
$v_f[t]=\mathrm{idf}(t)$ for $t\in T_{\le N}(f)$ and $0$ otherwise, then $L_2$-normalized,
\begin{equation}
\hat v_f = v_f/\lVert v_f\rVert_2. \label{eq:vector}
\end{equation}
The score of a product $p$ for a reference $q$ is the cosine of the normalized vectors, which
separates exactly into per-order contributions,
\begin{equation}
s(q,p)=\langle \hat v_q,\hat v_p\rangle=\sum_{k=1}^{N} S_k(q,p), \label{eq:score}
\end{equation}
where the order-$k$ term aggregates the shared order-$k$ tokens,
\begin{equation}
S_k(q,p)=\frac{1}{\lVert v_q\rVert\,\lVert v_p\rVert}\!\!\!\sum_{t\in T_k(q)\cap T_k(p)}\!\!\!\mathrm{idf}(t)^2. \label{eq:terms}
\end{equation}
Ranking the $340$ products by $s(q,p)$ and taking the highest few gives the recommendations.
For the order-three method, the order-three term supplies on average $54\%$ of the top-ranked
score, order two $37\%$, and order one only $9\%$: higher-order structure carries the majority
of the signal.

\subsection{Parameter-free design and explanations}
The matcher has no trained weights, so it cannot overfit---a principled choice on a catalog
with a single relevant product per query, where added capacity is later shown to degrade
accuracy. As a byproduct, the shared higher-order tokens of a recommendation, grouped by
Edwards-wheel super-family \cite{r10}, form auditable co-occurrence paths (\texttt{kg\_paths})
for a downstream explanation layer, in the spirit of path-based explainable recommendation
\cite{r21}; we report them as an artifact, not a faithfulness claim.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}
We ask three questions:
\begin{itemize}
\item[\textbf{RQ1.}] Does accord co-occurrence (order $\ge 2$) improve retrieval over the
order-one baseline?
\item[\textbf{RQ2.}] How far does the improvement continue with interaction order---is there a
saturation point?
\item[\textbf{RQ3.}] How does the order-three matcher compare with established paradigms:
marginal similarity, neural embeddings, perceptual transport, and learned models?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Setup}
The dataset and its non-circular firewall are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:firewall}; accord
lists are parsed by splitting on commas and semicolons, lower-casing, and trimming. The
order-$N$ matcher is compared against twelve methods spanning four paradigms: \emph{marginal}
similarity (Jaccard, TF-IDF cosine, BM25); \emph{neural} embeddings (multilingual
Sentence-BERT \cite{r22}, Word2Vec over accords, node2vec over the co-occurrence graph);
\emph{perceptual} transport (a tree-Wasserstein distance over the Edwards wheel \cite{r10},
following graph-based optimal transport \cite{r7,r9});
and \emph{learned} models (a PPMI--SVD embedding, a signature-subgraph ranker, a per-bigram
salience logistic model, a low-rank bilinear metric, and a gradient-boosting fusion). B2
(TF-IDF cosine) is the primary baseline. With one relevant product per query, quality is
measured by mean reciprocal rank (MRR) and Hits@$k$,
\begin{align}
\mathrm{MRR}&=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{Q}|}\sum_{q}\frac{1}{r_q},\\
\mathrm{Hits}@k&=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{Q}|}\sum_{q}\mathbf{1}[\,r_q\le k\,],\label{eq:metrics}
\end{align}
where $r_q$ is the rank of the ground-truth product and ties use the expected reciprocal rank
under uniform tie-breaking. All methods rank the full pool of $340$ products under
leave-one-out over the $209$ queries; learned rankers are scored out-of-fold with $5$-fold
GroupKFold grouped by query, and stochastic methods are averaged over $5$ seeds
(mean$\pm$std). Significance uses the Wilcoxon signed-rank test paired across queries on
reciprocal ranks, with $95\%$ confidence intervals from $10{,}000$ bootstrap resamples; the
text-based method is run after the leakage audit of Section~\ref{sec:problem}.

\subsection{RQ1 and RQ2: co-occurrence helps, then saturates}
Adding co-occurrence tokens raises accuracy sharply and then plateaus
(Fig.~\ref{fig:ablation}). MRR climbs from $0.454$ at order one to $0.496$ at order two and
$0.508$ at order three, and each step is statistically significant (order two vs.\ three:
$\Delta\mathrm{MRR}=0.012$, $p=2.8\times10^{-3}$). Beyond order three the curve is flat:
order four is not significantly better than order three ($\Delta\mathrm{MRR}=0.003$, $95\%$
CI $[-0.010,+0.005]$), so we adopt order three as the operating point. The plateau has a
simple cause: high-order co-occurrences become vanishingly rare, so little is left to
exploit---a query and its target share on average $4.1$ four-way, $1.2$ five-way, and $0.3$
six-way accord combinations, against $7.7$ triples. This makes the saturation point
dataset-dependent: a catalog with richer accord lists could extend the useful order.

\subsection{RQ3: order three versus established paradigms}
Table~\ref{tab:main} places order three against all twelve comparators. It ranks first on
every metric and beats each comparator significantly ($p<0.01$ throughout); the headline
margin over the marginal baseline B2 is $\Delta\mathrm{MRR}=+0.054$ ($p=5.6\times10^{-9}$).
Two regimes of margin stand apart. Over the simple, near-marginal methods the win is real but
small---$+0.054$ over TF-IDF (B2), $+0.064$ over PPMI--SVD (A2), $+0.051$ over gradient
boosting (A6), and $+0.080$ over Jaccard (B1)---so these methods approach without surpassing
the co-occurrence signal. Over the complex models the win is decisive: $+0.236$ over
Sentence-BERT (B4), $+0.323$ over Word2Vec (B5), $+0.358$ over the bilinear metric (A5), and
$+0.369$ over per-bigram salience (A4). Tellingly, the two parametric models that explicitly
try to \emph{learn} co-occurrence weights---per-bigram salience and the bilinear metric---overfit
the worst and collapse to the bottom of the table under the one-positive-per-query imbalance.
Exact, unweighted co-occurrence thus beats \emph{learned} co-occurrence here, which is why the
parameter-free form is a design decision, not a limitation. The weak Sentence-BERT result is
only partial evidence against neural semantics: query and product text are asymmetric---sparse
accord lists against rich Indonesian prose---so B4 is penalized by a text mismatch independent
of its semantic quality.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
This work studied interaction-free, content-based perfume recommendation and asked whether
modeling accord co-occurrence improves retrieval over the marginal similarity that dominates
the field. The contribution is a finding and a protocol, not a new model. The task is cast as
retrieval behind a non-circular evaluation---a three-source firewall with externally verified
query accords, a leakage audit, and a comparison against twelve methods across four
paradigms---and the reason interaction-driven recommenders (KGAT, LightGCN, KGIN) cannot be
instantiated on a catalog without user interactions is made explicit. Within this protocol, a
deliberately parameter-free matcher that pools accord co-occurrences up to order three ranks
first, beating every comparator; accuracy rises with interaction order and then saturates, so
order three is the operating point. The headline gain over the TF-IDF baseline is modest but
robust ($\Delta\mathrm{MRR}=+0.054$, $p=5.6\times10^{-9}$), and higher-order structure carries
most of the signal (order three alone supplies $54\%$ of the top-ranked score). Adding a
learning-to-rank layer over this representation yields no significant improvement ($p=0.18$),
so the parameter-free form is retained.

The result is reported with its limits intact. The effect is modest in absolute terms, and all
data come from a single proprietary catalog in one market, so external validity remains
untested. Accords are binary, without per-accord intensity, so products with identical accord
sets cannot be distinguished, and because the imitation relation is a perceptual proxy rather
than a measure of user satisfaction, its already high query--target accord overlap---about
$86\%$---imposes a ceiling intrinsic to the task that bounds the achievable gain. The advantage
across paradigms is, to our knowledge, the first non-circular demonstration of its kind, yet it
is claimed only for this setting, and the high-order plateau itself reflects the scarcity of
shared high-order tokens in this catalog and may shift with richer accord vocabularies. These
limits point to natural next steps: intensity-weighted co-occurrence, a symmetric-text
evaluation to isolate the semantic contribution of sentence encoders, a counterfactual
edge-ablation study to quantify the faithfulness of the exported \texttt{kg\_paths}, and
replication across further catalogs and domains.
